Best time to visit

The best time is in the shoulder season, which is April-May and September-October. During these months the weather is still warm enough and you'll be able to beat the summer crowds.

Depending on the year, there may still be snow in May and it might start snowing as early as September, so do check the weather before you go.

Be aware that during the winter season some of the mountains in the Dolomites are used as ski slopes.

Where to stay

The Dolomites is located across three Northern Italian regions and five Italian provinces. Those three regions are Veneto, Trentino Alto-Adige/Südtirol, and Friuli Venezia Giulia. It is an enormous alpine area and trying to decide where to stay can be a little overwhelming.

When trying to decide where to stay, ask yourself what are the hikes/sites you would like to see and how many days will you be staying in the Dolomites.

The popular locations aren't near each other therefore there are a few options on where to stay

Stay in different areas and move every night - 

Pros you'll be near the attractions,

  • Cons It's pretty tiring to move from one hotel to another, every night.
  • Stay in 2 different areas - It’s far better to choose two, where you can divide your time equally.
  • Base yourself in one area but expect to do a lot of driving. That is what we did.

1. Val Gardena, South Tyrol

There are three towns in Val Gardena/Grodnertal Ortisei, Santa Cristina, and Selva di Val Gardena.

This is where we stayed and here are a few reasons why

  • Free public transit card When sleeping anywhere in Val Gardena you're given a free public transit card. This transit card means you can get to various trailheads without having to worry about driving and paying for parking.
  • There are many restaurants.
  • Attractions nearby From Val Gardena, you can easily reach the Seceda, Val di Funes, and Alpe di Siusi (Seiser Alm). Lago di Carezza can also be reached within an hour from here.

 

2. Val di Funes, South Tyrol

Val di Funes, aka Villnöss, is a must-see while visiting the Dolomites. This valley is perfect for those looking for a beautiful view of the dramatic Geisler/Odle peaks and peaceful, offbeat region.

  • There are many great hikes around the area.
  • Attractions nearby From Val di Funes you can reach the Seceda, Val di Funes, and Alpe di Siusi (Seiser Alm) within 40 minutes.
  • Cons Don't expect there to be many restaurants here, there isn't much in Val di Funes besides nature.

 

3. Alpe di Siusi, South Tyrol

Alpe di Siusi is a magical place to be for sunrise or sunset, but this can be a bit complicated to reach if you are unaware of the rules. 

If you're looking to drive up the roads are closed between 9 am to 5 pm. You can drive up to Compatsch before and after these hours. It's about an hour's walk to the most picturesque part of Alpe di Siusi. The cable car starts working at 8 am.

The best way to actually witness the sunset or sunrise with the least effort is staying at a hotel on the mountain.

 

 4. Bolzano, Trentino Alto-Adige

It's a big city in Trentino which is tucked away in the Italian Alps. Here you will find stunning old churches and ancient castles.

  • Easily reachable by car and public transportation.
  • Attractions nearby From Bolzano, you can reach the Seceda, Val di Funes, and Alpe di Siusi (Seiser Alm) within an hour.
  • Cons Not central in the Dolomites

5. Cortina d’Ampezzo, Belluno

It's a big town, considered the heart of the Southern Alps. It's filled with restaurants, shopping, museums, bars, and nightlife.

  • Some of the best nearby day hikes include Lago di Sorapis, Tre Cime di Lavaredo, Misurina, and a walk around the famous Lago di Braies.
  • It's accessible by car and public transportation.
  • Cons It is further away from sites like Lake Braies, Alpe di Siusi, and Lago di Carezza.

Things to know before your trip

1. Language

In the Dolomites, most places have two different names, because a large area of the Dolomites is located in a region that was part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire until WWI when it officially became part of Italy. For this reason, until today, most places in the region of Alto Adige (in Italian) / South Tyrol (in German) have two different names, one in each language.

 

2. South Tyrolians

Those who live in South Tyrol don't consider themselves Italian nor German, They Prefer to be called South Tyrolians. If you call them German or Italian, they may find it offensive.

 

3. Rent a car

The best way to get around in the Dolomites is by car! Getting around by public transportation isn't easy in the Dolomites and if you truly want to experience this area to its fullest you need to rent a car.

 

4. Weather

The Dolomite Mountains offer some of the best weather in all of the Alps, perfect for year-round. Saying that it is important to always check the forecast beforehand and don’t underestimate the weather.  Take into consideration that the valleys will be warmer while the mountains will get colder and could get very foggy.

  • Summer (June-September): Warm temperatures and plenty of sun which is perfect for any activity. While the average maximum temperature may reach some 25 degrees Celsius, there is a nice breeze.
  • Fall (September-November): The temperature drops, but weather conditions remain stable and pleasant.
  • Winter (December-March): Snow usually begins to accumulate during these months and is very popular for those looking to do winter sports.
  • Spring (April-May): During these months it gets warmer, but it also rains. It's a great time to visit since the spring brings beautiful green valleys and pastures overflowing with wildflowers.

 

5. Gear

Hiking in the Dolomites can be challenging, even the most experienced hiker may find the hikes there challenging. Don’t underestimate the importance of good and sturdy hiking boots. Hiking sticks can also come in handy since some of the trails are narrow, have small slippery rocks, and have sheer drops with nowhere to hold unto.

Also, since the weather is unpredictable, I would use the onion layer method (layered outfits are your best friend).

 

6. Avoid crowds

The Dolomites has become famous over the years (thanks to social media), therefore expect crowds even during the early mornings. Wake up as early as possible so you can really enjoy the beautiful nature without crowds.

 

7. Best time for photography

There is something about the sunsets/sunrises in the Alps, they are almost pink. If you are looking to capture beautiful photos in the Dolomites, aim for sunrise and sunsets.

 

8. Carry small change

The Dolomites has small villages and most places don't accept credit cards, cash only.
